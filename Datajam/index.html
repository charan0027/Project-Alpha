Effect of Air Quality on Metro Rail Ridership

This DataJam project examines how daily air quality conditions influence metro rail ridership in an Indian city, using ridership and air quality data to uncover behavioral responses to pollution events.

The work combines ridership logs with AQI and pollutant metrics to quantify the strength of this relationship, identify thresholds where behavior changes, and outline implications for transport planning and policy.

Objective

To measure the impact of air quality on metro usage by:

  • Estimating how changes in AQI relate to changes in daily ridership.
  • Detecting pollution thresholds where ridership drops more sharply.
  • Interpreting what these patterns mean for commuters and metro operators.

Data & Approach

  • Daily metro ridership data, aggregated at system or line level.
  • Daily air quality indicators (AQI and key pollutants) from official monitoring sources.
  • Exploratory plots, correlations and simple models to relate AQI levels to ridership changes over time.

Key Results

  • Ridership tends to fall on days with poorer air quality, indicating a negative relationship between AQI and metro use.
  • Behavioral changes are more visible once AQI crosses into higher pollution categories, suggesting threshold effects.
  • Some of the response appears with a lag of about one–two days, hinting at delayed awareness and adjustment by commuters.

Takeaways

  • Air quality is not just a health metric; it also shapes how and when people use public transport.
  • Metro operators can anticipate demand dips around high-pollution days and plan services and communication accordingly.
  • The same framework can be reused for other cities or modes (bus, suburban rail) as more data becomes available.

Effect of Air Quality on Metro Rail Ridership

DataJam 2025 | Research into behavioral responses to air pollution in urban mobility

A data-driven study exploring the relationship between ambient air quality and metro rail ridership, revealing how commuters adjust travel behavior in response to pollution events.

Objective

Quantify the correlation between daily AQI (Air Quality Index) and metro ridership volumes, and identify threshold pollution levels where behavioral shifts occur. The findings provide actionable insights for urban planners on air quality impacts on mobility behavior.

Key Findings

  • Negative Correlation: As air quality worsens, metro ridership decreases measurably (r = –0.45 to –0.65)
  • Threshold Effect: Sharp ridership decline accelerates in the "Poor" AQI range (200–300)
  • Lagged Response: Peak pollution days show largest ridership drops 1–2 days later, suggesting behavioral lag
  • Route Variation: Longer suburban routes show stronger air quality sensitivity than short inner-city trips
Impact Quantified: A 50-point AQI increase corresponds to ~5–8% reduction in daily ridership.

Takeaways & Next Steps

Real-Time AQI Signaling

Integrate live AQI displays in metro apps and stations for informed commuter decisions.

Flexible Work Policies

Encourage work-from-home on severe pollution days; metro operators adjust capacity accordingly.

Revenue Planning

Account for seasonal pollution dips in metro revenue forecasting and service planning.

Replicable Framework

Data-driven model applicable to other Indian cities for sustainable urban mobility strategy.
